Last year I had a brain MRI done as part of a research study at the University of Melbourne [Centre for Brain, Mind and Markets](https://www.unimelb.edu.au/cbmm/). Afterwards, the researchers sent me the raw scan so I could see inside my own brain. At first this mostly felt uncanny. Then it became irresistible. If you are given a dense measurement of your own brain, what exactly can you do with it?

This post is my attempt to answer that question.

The interactive model at the top is part of the answer, but it is not really the point. The point is that the same scan could be explored in several different ways: as raw slices, as segmented anatomy, as labelled structures, as measurements, and eventually as a set of objects I could rotate in the browser.

## The raw scan is fascinating but not very helpful

As a medical student, I've been taught the basics of interpreting brain MRIs, so opening up the raw scan in a MRI slice viewer was quite underwhelming. It was essentially the same as any other MRI I've seen along the course of my education.

However, in my case, having the scan file itself let me extract a bit more information about how it was made:

<BrainScanFacts items={brainScanFacts} />

Some of this I already knew from looking up the machine they used to scan me - the Siemens Magnetom 7T Plus Ultra High Field MRI scanner at the [Melbourne Brain Centre Imaging Unit](https://medicine.unimelb.edu.au/school-structure/medicine/research/mbciu#equipment), one of two 7 Tesla scanners in Australia. However, finding out the dimensions was useful because they set the ceiling for everything that follows. A higher-quality structural scan makes later steps much less miserable. If I had started with a much blurrier clinical scan, a lot of the structure-level exploration below would have been harder to pull off cleanly.[^2]

The most immediate way to inspect the MRI was just to scroll through it in the three standard anatomical planes.

## Turning voxels into anatomy

The first serious leap was to run the scan through [FreeSurfer](https://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/fswiki/FreeSurferWiki), which is one of the standard toolkits for cortical reconstruction and anatomical segmentation.[^3]

I had never used this kind of software before, but immediately upon installing it, I realised how complex and powerful it was for neuroimaging research. Essentially, it could take a grayscale set of brain slices and tell me:

- where is the cortex?
- where is the white matter?
- where do the ventricles begin and end?
- which deep structures are likely to be thalamus, hippocampus, caudate, amygdala, and so on?

This is helpfully all done within the one [`recon-all` command](https://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/fswiki/recon-all), which is designed to extract as much information as possible from my brain.

<FreeSurferRuntimeChart client:visible items={freesurferRuntimeStages} />

It took 12 discrete steps and four hours for the program to essentially:

1. Remove my skull, neck and eyes (ouch)
2. Align the scan to FreeSurfer's own anatomical templates and priors
3. Estimate where the major tissue boundaries and structures are likely to be, accounting for my individual anatomy

By the end of that process, the scan was a detailed, computationally labelled model of my brain, full of named tissues and structures. More importantly, this became the main anatomical backbone of the project: the best subject-specific reconstruction I had, and the one I would eventually use for the 3D model. One surprise was that the software produced not just shapes, but quantities, so I could see objectively how big my brain was.

<BrainCompartmentChart client:visible items={brainCompartmentVolumes} />

This was one of the first moments where the scan started to feel interpretable, but more work was required before I could intuitively understand where the different parts of my brain were placed.

## Pulling structures out one by one

FreeSurfer gave me the better anatomical reconstruction, but I also wanted a simpler, more readable way to inspect the scan structure by structure. So alongside the FreeSurfer pipeline, I built a second pass that warped the Harvard-Oxford atlas into my scan. It was not as subject-specific as FreeSurfer, which is exactly why I did not use it as the main source of final anatomy. But it was extremely useful for overlays, quick snapshots, and checking whether the labels were landing where I expected.

Visualising that atlas-based pass, we get something a bit more interpretable than the original slices:

## Where exactly does the hippocampus end?

The most interesting thing about this sort of exploration is that it eventually forces you to notice uncertainty.

Medical images do not come with glowing built-in outlines around every structure. The software has to infer boundaries from contrast, spatial priors, atlas information, geometry, and a fair amount of accumulated anatomical opinion.

That becomes obvious as soon as you compare the stronger, more subject-specific FreeSurfer reconstruction against the cruder but more explanatory atlas-based labeling pass.

<AtlasComparisonChart client:visible items={atlasVsFreesurferVolumes} />

I found this chart much more interesting than I expected.

At first glance it looks like a fight: atlas labels versus FreeSurfer labels, who wins? In one sense, FreeSurfer probably does win here — it is the better reconstruction pipeline, and the one I trusted more for the main anatomy. But that is not really the most interesting thing that the chart shows. The more useful interpretation is that both pipelines broadly agree about *what is where*, while still disagreeing about some borders — especially for smaller, fuzzier or more model-dependent structures.

That is a genuinely good thing to learn from your own data. It stops the whole project from becoming a naïve “computer sees the truth” story, and it justifies keeping the atlas-based pass around even though it was not the better final model.

The hippocampus is a good example. Everyone has heard of it. Everyone is primed to care about it. But when you try to segment it computationally, you discover that even a structure with a famous name can have boundaries that depend heavily on method. The amygdala makes the same point even more aggressively. By the time I got to those smaller structures, the lesson had become pretty clear: the scan contains evidence, but not a full set of pre-existing lines.

In other words, one of the things my MRI let me explore was not just anatomy, but the limits of anatomical certainty.

## What the software was willing to measure

Once I had labels and volumes, I could also start treating the scan as a source of quantitative curiosities.

Some of these were broad and unsurprising, like the compartment volumes above. Others were smaller and more personal-feeling, even if they were still just outputs of a pipeline rather than anything clinically important. For example, the scan estimated a slight left-right cortical asymmetry:

- **left cortex:** 262,938 mm³
- **right cortex:** 257,845 mm³

That is not the sort of number I would want to over-interpret. But I still find it deeply satisfying that the software is willing to hand it over. The same applies to all the left-right pairs scattered through the deep structures. Once the scan became a set of named regions, it stopped being only an image and became a database of anatomical guesses.

That was one of the recurring pleasures of the project. Every time I thought I had exhausted the scan, another possible question appeared:

- Can I label this structure?
- Can I measure it?
- Can I compare two methods on it?
- Can I turn it into something I can inspect spatially?

## Turning anatomy into objects

Eventually, of course, I started turning some of those labelled structures into 3D objects.

This was not the main intellectual point of the project, but it was too fun to avoid. Once the anatomy existed as explicit, named pieces, I could extract surfaces for individual regions and turn them into browser-friendly objects. That is where the widget at the top came from.

I want to be a little careful about the framing here, because the widget is not meant to be a full medical viewer. It is more like a souvenir of the rest of the exploration.

The important transition was not “I used Three.js”. The important transition was that the scan stopped being a stack of slices and became something I could split apart, rotate, label and inspect as discrete anatomical pieces. Most of those final objects came from the FreeSurfer-derived anatomy; the atlas-based pass mainly helped me explain and sanity-check what I was looking at. That changes the feel of the data completely. A hippocampus buried in a grayscale volume is one thing; a hippocampus you can pull away from the rest of the brain and rotate in space is another.

So the widget earned its place in the post, but as a companion rather than a conclusion. It is a compressed summary of the exploration, not the exploration itself.

## What I actually got out of this

I think the reason I kept coming back to the scan is that it kept changing what kind of object it was.

At first it was just a weirdly intimate data file. Then it was a set of slices. Then it was a segmented anatomical model. Then it was a collection of structures with names and volumes. Then it was a set of objects I could move around in the browser.

Each version was more interpretable than the last, but also more mediated. The raw slices are closest to the scanner, and hardest to understand. The labelled structures are much easier to reason about, but also much more processed, inferred and opinionated. That tension is probably the most interesting thing in the whole project.

So if you ask me what I did with the MRI, the honest answer is not just “I made a widget”.

I used it to explore how much of a brain can be pulled out of data before you stop looking at a scan and start looking at a model.

And I think that is a much more interesting outcome.
